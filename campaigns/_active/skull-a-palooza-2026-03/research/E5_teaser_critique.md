## 🔴 BEN SETTLE

This is A-tier work. I want to say that up front before I start pulling things apart.

The Jamie scene is the best piece of character writing in this entire campaign. And I don't mean that as a craft compliment — I mean it as a SALES compliment. Because while everyone is reading about Jamie timing his packing like an Olympic event, they're absorbing proof that this sale is REAL. The warehouse is ready. The team is excited. The infrastructure is in place. That's credibility wrapped in entertainment. Textbook infotainment.

### Line-by-Line

**1. The customer quotes section needs rawer energy:**

> "Texted. I'm in. Already set a Tuesday reminder."
> "Thank you for bringing the $9.95 deal back. I missed it last time and I'm still mad about it."
> "I can't wait for Tuesday to come."

These read like testimonials on a landing page. Too polished. The WE1 email worked because the customer quote was imperfect — "is this clickbait?" — THAT sounds like a real person. These sound like you wrote them. If they're real, make them messier. Add a typo. Include the name. One raw quote > three polished ones.

**Suggested fix — pick ONE and make it live:**

> The replies I've been getting all week say it all. One witch texted back: "I missed the $9.95 deal last time and I'm still mad about it. I already set 3 alarms for Tuesday."
>
> Three alarms. For a handbag sale. I love my customers.

One quote. A reaction. Done.

**2. The transition from quotes → Jamie is clunky:**

> "And it's not just the inbox. The warehouse has that same energy right now, thanks to one person you'd least expect to care about leather skull goods."

This is an announcer voice. It says "now I'm going to tell you about Jamie." Compare to how the WE1 email transitioned — Julie made a decision ("Fine. Let me open the vault."). Here Julie is narrating a structure.

**Suggested fix:**

> But the craziest energy this week hasn't been in my inbox. It's been in the warehouse. And it's coming from someone who could not care LESS about skull aesthetics.

Shorter. More intrigue. Sounds like Julie, not a host.

**3. "real leather skull goods" — watch the "real leather" phrasing:**

Same note from WE1 — use "premium" or just "leather skull goods." Don't need "real."

This email has the best scene in the campaign. Full stop.

| **Dialogue** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | "ADEQUATE? I was a MACHINE." / "Why can't it be both?" / "...Fine. It sounds a LITTLE fun." — This is the best dialogue in the entire campaign. Jamie and Alice are instantly real. The comedic timing is professional-grade. |
| **Show vs Tell** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | The warehouse scene is all SHOW. Beautiful. But the opening (inbox replies) is all TELL. "My inbox has been one word on repeat" is Julie summarizing — not showing us a moment. |
| **Emotional Pacing** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | The arc is: social proof → warehouse scene → urgency → CTA. It works. But the social proof section and the warehouse scene are two separate mini-emails stitched together. The best Daniel Throssell emails feel like ONE story with a pivot. This feels like two stories. |
| **Specificity** | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | "20 units in 12 minutes." "4 hours long. Timed to a full packing shift." "Speaker in the corner." "Playlist called 'Skull-a-palooza Packing Focus.'" — Every single detail is specific, concrete, and believable. This is what makes the scene work. |

### Line-by-Line

**1. The playlist reveal is PERFECT — don't change a word:**

> And playing from a speaker in the corner: a playlist called "Skull-a-palooza Packing Focus."
> "Did you make a playlist?"
> "4 hours long. Timed to a full packing shift. You're welcome."

This is scene-stacking at its best. The physical detail (speaker in the corner) grounds us in space. Then the dialogue reveals character. Then the specificity ("4 hours long") makes it impossible to think this is made up. Chef's kiss.

**2. The "So when I walked in yesterday" section is the strongest paragraph in the email:**

> bags were organized by design and lined up in rows, packing tables wiped clean, wrapping paper cut and stacked, tape guns loaded, labels ready.

This is a LIST but it works because each item escalates the sense of preparation. It's not random — it's a widening shot of a warehouse that's READY. The reader absorbs: this is real, this is happening, these people take this seriously.

**3. The P.S. is doing exactly what a P.S. should do:**

> Alice texted me at midnight last night: "I can't believe I'm saying this but... I'm a little excited." Don't tell Jamie.

This is the emotional payoff. Alice, who spent the entire email being the skeptic, privately admits she's excited. The "don't tell Jamie" is the perfect landing — it keeps the dynamic alive and makes the reader feel like they just got a secret. Very strong.

**4. My ONE structural concern:**

The email is really two emails glued together:

- **Part 1 (lines 1-12):** Inbox social proof + CTA
- **Part 2 (lines 13-end):** Jamie warehouse scene + CTA

They're connected thematically but not narratively. The reader has to "restart" when Jamie appears. In my best emails, the opening moment and the closing moment are part of the SAME story. Here they're parallel.

**Possible fix:** What if you cut the customer quotes entirely and OPENED with the warehouse? Julie walks in, sees Jamie's setup, and that becomes the vehicle for everything — the prices, the urgency, the CTA. The customer replies could become a one-line aside ("700+ witches are texting me like it's Christmas").

But honestly? This works as-is. It's a Teaser #5 — you're consolidating proof from multiple angles. Two scenes is defensible.
