## The G.A.M.E.S.H.O.W. Email Marketing Framework

The **G.A.M.E.S.H.O.W. Email Marketing Framework** is designed for executing a fun, engaging, and **PROFITABLE** limited-time campaign. This framework was retrofitted after a reader compared the experience of receiving the author's emails during the campaign to watching a "game show".

### Campaign Background and Results

The framework was developed following the successful execution of **Daniel Throssell’s Black Friday MADNESS!** campaign.

1.  **Inspiration:** The campaign was adapted from two similar sales strategies:
    *   **Jon Rimmerman's daily wine emails:** Specifically, the "Early Bird Gets The Wine" sale, which involved sending over 10 emails with highly-limited wine offers over the space of a few hours before Black Friday.
    *   **Ben Settle’s “Seasons Emailings”:** A series of discounts on one of his books offered every 18 hours over several days.
2.  **Premise:** For the Black Friday MADNESS campaign, the author introduced a new rare or hard-to-find offer from the catalogue every day for several days. These offers were only available for **24 hours**, after which they were replaced by the next limited-time offer.
3.  **Outcome:** The campaign was a **HUGE success**. It was highly profitable, generating more revenue in one week than the author previously earned in a year at a high-paid engineering job. Crucially, the campaign achieved this while *improving the relationship* with the email list by providing entertainment, engagement, and excitement.

### The Eight Principles of G.A.M.E.S.H.O.W.

The framework is structured around eight core principles:

#### G. Offer A Genuine Bargain
Marketers often spend too much energy trying to make an average offer *seem* irresistible by "shout[ing] and scream[ing] and us[ing] trickery". Instead, the author advises offering a deal so good it **ACTUALLY makes you uncomfortable**.

*   **Core Philosophy:** All the author's best offers have **GENUINELY made [him] feel like [he] was being an idiot for charging so little**.
*   **Payoffs:** Offering an insane bargain, like launching the *Email Storyteller* course for $333 when it could have sold for $1,000+, entertains and delights customers, keeps them in the habit of buying, and trains them to associate buying with happy emotions, leading them to eagerly anticipate and not miss future emails.

#### A. Don’t Be Ashamed Of Selling
This principle champions **pure, hard, SHAMELESS selling**. Selling should be done aggressively and with commitment, without apologizing or making self-deprecating remarks about running a sale.

*   **Customer Desire:** Consumers do not like to be "virtue-signalled to" (e.g., marketers mocking sales). **THEY WANT TO BE SOOOOOLD**.
*   **Selling is Entertainment:** Selling is not evil; it is not a bad thing. **GOOD SELLING IS ENTERTAINING**.

#### M. Be (A Little) Mad
The willingness to portray oneself as a little **CRAZY!** is cited as a "secret weapon" in selling.

*   **The Narrative:** If people perceive the seller as crazy, they are more likely to believe that the deal being offered **IS an irrationally good bargain**. The narrative "I’m doing this deal just because I’m insane" works as an excellent all-purpose reason for any awesome deal.
*   **Application:** The campaign was named “Black Friday MADNESS”. For example, when offering the *Sales Page Ecademay* for just **$50** (a product containing 11+ hours of content that did not meet the author's $1,000 product standards yet), the author used manic energy and talked in the third person to emphasize the insanity of the price. The $1 JACKPOT offer was also meant to make the author look crazy and create an excellent spectacle.

#### E. Make It An Event
Transforming a sale into an **EVENT** is a powerful tactic. An 'event' is defined by three elements: **1. A name; 2. A clearly defined start/end; 3. Is never, or rarely, repeated**. The Black Friday MADNESS campaign utilized this by naming the series of offers, repeating the name in each email, and ensuring it had a defined time period and rarity.

#### S. Lean On Scarcity
While time scarcity (the 24-hour deadline) was employed, the author believes **scarcity of quantity** is fundamentally more powerful than scarcity of time. Quantity scarcity (e.g., "only 5 available") evokes a more primal urge.

*   **Application:** The success of the campaign relied on including offers that had **real, genuine QUANTITY limits**. Examples include the custom art offer (limited to three copies at $500 each) and the JACKPOT (limited to a sole winner). The inclusion of quantity-limited bargains was the single biggest reason people described the campaign as a “game show”.

#### H. Be Hard to Predict
To prevent disengagement, the campaign must be **Unpredictable**. If people don't know what is coming next, they are inclined to keep tuning in.

*   **Methods of Unpredictability:** The author maximized unpredictability by mixing multiple dimensions:
    1.  Mixing up offer types (Bonus Levels, custom art, new products).
    2.  Mixing up price points (from the $1 JACKPOT to the $500 custom art).
    3.  Mixing up availability (capped vs. uncapped).
    4.  Mixing up the way to obtain offers (buying vs. winning the JACKPOT).
    5.  Giving **no hints** about upcoming offers.

#### O. Stay Offer Focused
This principle states that the entire campaign is founded on the strength of the offer. The author emailed his list for an entire week **without offering an OUNCE of “value”** in the traditional sense, yet the list "ABSOLUTELY LOVED IT".

*   **The Foundation:** Offers are the entire bedrock of the G.A.M.E.S.H.O.W. framework. If you have an unbelievably **sexy OFFER**, people will be too busy **WANTING** it to care how much or how hard you are selling it to them. They will willingly read every single word until the deadline.

#### W. Wing It!
The author asserts that campaigns will **ALWAYS WORK BETTER WHEN YOU RUN THEM LIVE**. Running live allows for adaptation "on the fly" based on audience feedback, sales, and events.

*   **Adaptation Examples:**
    1.  The original plan was only to offer rare/hard-to-find items from the existing catalogue. However, after customers complained there was nothing new for them, the author adapted to introduce the custom art offer and two brand new products (*Sales Page Ecademay* and *Email Storyteller*). This adaptation likely made "tens of thousands of dollars more".
    2.  When the $1 JACKPOT offer was cracked by a programmer, the author reopened the offer mid-way through the 24-hour period to add a twist, resulting in a feel-good story for the eventual winner.
*   **Conclusion:** Resisting the temptation to pre-write allows campaigns to be exposed to serendipity, leading to better results.
